The law of inertia

First Law (Inertia)

"An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion with the same speed and in the same direction unless acted upon by a net force."

This is called the law of inertia. Inertia is the tendency of objects to resist changes in their state of motion.

Mistake: Thinking objects "want" to stop moving
Tip: Objects naturally continue moving - friction and air resistance cause them to stop!
Mistake: Confusing inertia with momentum
Tip: Inertia is the resistance to change, momentum is mass √ó velocity

Side Quest 3.2: Newton's Second Law

F = ma

Historical Context: The Birth of Dynamics

Isaac Newton (1643-1727): Developed the second law as part of his comprehensive theory of motion.

Leonhard Euler (1707-1783): Later formalized F = ma as the fundamental equation of dynamics.

Impact: This law enabled engineers to calculate forces needed for bridges, buildings, and machinery!

Second Law: F = ma

"The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass."

F = ma, where F = net force (N), m = mass (kg), a = acceleration (m/s¬≤)

Real-World Applications

üèóÔ∏è Construction: Engineers use F = ma to design safe building foundations and bridges
üöó Automotive: Car designers calculate braking forces needed for safe stopping distances
üèÉ Sports Science: Coaches analyze athlete acceleration using force-mass relationships

Key Formulas

F = ma (Newton's Second Law)

Weight: W = mg (force of gravity)

Common Mistakes & Tips

Mistake: Forgetting that F is NET force
Tip: Always calculate the vector sum of all forces acting on the object!
Mistake: Confusing mass and weight
Tip: Mass is constant (kg), weight depends on gravity (N)!

F = ma = 1000 √ó 2 = 2000 N

Side Quest 3.3: Newton's Third Law

Action and reaction

Historical Context: Newton's Complete Picture

Isaac Newton (1643-1727): The third law completed his comprehensive theory of motion.

Impact: This law explains everything from rocket propulsion to walking - forces always come in pairs!

Third Law: Action-Reaction

"For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction."

Forces always come in pairs. If object A exerts a force on object B, then object B exerts an equal and opposite force on object A.

Real-World Applications

üöÄ Rocket Propulsion: Rockets push exhaust gases backward, gases push rocket forward
üèÉ Walking: Your foot pushes backward on the ground, ground pushes you forward
üèÄ Sports: Basketball players push the ball, ball pushes back on their hand

Common Mistakes & Tips

Mistake: Thinking action-reaction forces cancel
Tip: Action-reaction forces act on DIFFERENT objects, so they never cancel!
Mistake: Confusing with balanced forces
Tip: Balanced forces act on the SAME object, action-reaction on DIFFERENT objects!

Practice Exercise

Problem: A book rests on a table. Identify the action-reaction pair between the book and table.

Solution:
Action: Book pushes DOWN on table (force of weight)
Reaction: Table pushes UP on book (normal force)
These forces are equal and opposite, acting on different objects!
Hint: Look for forces between two different objects!
