Side Quest 6.1: Hydrocarbons

Alkanes, alkenes, alkynes

Historical Context

Friedrich Wöhler (1828) - Synthesized urea from inorganic compounds, challenging vitalism.

August Kekulé (1858) - Developed structural theory and carbon tetravalency.

Jacobus van 't Hoff (1874) - Proposed tetrahedral carbon atom model.

Hydrocarbons

Alkanes: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ (single bonds only)

Alkenes: CₙH₂ₙ (at least one C=C)

Alkynes: CₙH₂ₙ₋₂ (at least one C≡C)

Real-World Applications

Petroleum Industry - Hydrocarbons are the basis of fuels and plastics
Pharmaceuticals - Many drugs are based on hydrocarbon structures
Materials Science - Polymers and synthetic materials from hydrocarbons

Practice Exercise

Problem: Name and classify the compound: CH₃-CH₂-CH=CH₂

Solution:

Name: But-1-ene

Classification: Alkene (contains C=C double bond)

Structure: 4 carbon chain with double bond between C1 and C2

Molecular formula: C₄H₈

Common Mistakes & Tips

Mistake: Confusing alkane, alkene, and alkyne formulas

Tip: Alkane: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂, Alkene: CₙH₂ₙ, Alkyne: CₙH₂ₙ₋₂

Mistake: Forgetting to number carbon chains correctly

Tip: Number from end closest to functional group/double bond

Side Quest 6.2: Functional Groups

OH, carbonyl, carboxyl

Functional Groups

Alcohol: -OH (e.g., ethanol)

Carbonyl: C=O (aldehydes/ketones)

Carboxyl: -COOH (acids)
