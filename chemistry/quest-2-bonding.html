üìú Historical Context

Svante Arrhenius (1884) - First proposed ionic theory explaining how salts dissociate into ions in water.

Gilbert N. Lewis (1916) - Developed electron pair theory and octet rule, explaining ionic bond formation.

Linus Pauling (1930s) - Applied quantum mechanics to chemical bonding, quantifying ionic character.

Side Quest 2.2: Covalent Bonding

Non-metal + Non-metal share

üîí

üìú Historical Context

Gilbert N. Lewis (1916) - Proposed covalent bonding as electron pair sharing between atoms.

Irving Langmuir (1919) - Expanded Lewis theory and coined term "covalent bond."

Linus Pauling (1930s) - Developed valence bond theory and introduced concept of hybridization.

üìö Covalent Bonds

Single: 2 electrons shared (H-H)

Double: 4 electrons shared (O=O)

Triple: 6 electrons shared (N‚â°N)

Equal sharing = non-polar. Unequal sharing = polar covalent.

Water (H‚ÇÇO) - Polar covalent bonds enable hydrogen bonding, essential for life
Plastics & Polymers - Long chains of covalently bonded carbon atoms
Pharmaceuticals - Covalent bonds create stable drug molecules

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Mistakes & Tips

Mistake: Thinking covalent bonds are weaker than ionic bonds (some covalent bonds are very strong)

Mistake: Forgetting that covalent bonds can be polar or non-polar

Tip: Check electronegativity difference - if >0.4, bond is polar covalent

Metallic bonds involve a "sea" of delocalized electrons.
