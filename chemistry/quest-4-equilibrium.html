Side Quest 4.1: Equilibrium Basics

Dynamic equilibrium

Historical Context

Cato Guldberg & Peter Waage (1864) - Developed law of mass action and equilibrium constant concept.

Henry Le Chatelier (1884) - Formulated principle predicting equilibrium shifts.

Jacobus van 't Hoff (1880s) - Applied thermodynamics to chemical equilibrium.

Equilibrium

Reversible reactions: aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD

At equilibrium: rate forward = rate reverse

Kc = [C]^c[D]^d / [A]^a[B]^b

Real-World Applications

Haber Process - Industrial ammonia production using equilibrium principles
Buffer Solutions - Maintain pH stability in biological systems
Oxygen Transport - Hemoglobin-oxygen equilibrium in blood

Practice Exercise

Problem: For the reaction N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃, Kc = 0.5 at 400°C. If [N₂] = 0.2 M, [H₂] = 0.6 M, is the system at equilibrium?

Solution: Calculate Q = [NH₃]² / ([N₂][H₂]³)

Q = (0.1)² / (0.2 × 0.6³) = 0.01 / (0.2 × 0.216) = 0.01 / 0.0432 ≈ 0.23

Since Q < Kc (0.23 < 0.5), reaction will shift right to reach equilibrium.

Common Mistakes & Tips

Mistake: Thinking equilibrium means concentrations are equal

Tip: Equilibrium means rates are equal, not concentrations

Mistake: Forgetting to raise concentrations to their coefficients

Tip: Always include stoichiometric coefficients in Kc expression

Side Quest 4.2: Le Chatelier's Principle

Shifting equilibrium

Le Chatelier's Principle

Concentration: Increase → shift away

Temperature: Exothermic + heat → shifts left

Pressure: Increase → shift to fewer moles

  • A) Forward
  • B) Backward
A: B Adding heat to exothermic shifts to absorb it (left).
